Section 02 · Foundations

Understanding the restaurant industry

To sell to restaurant owners, you have to see the world through their eyes.

Most restaurant owners are extraordinary at running a restaurant — and stretched far too thin to run a marketing operation. Understanding their reality is what makes you credible.

The reality of most restaurant owners

  • They work long, exhausting hours — often open to close.
  • They have little to no time for marketing.
  • They post inconsistently — a flurry one week, silence the next.
  • Their content is outdated — sometimes years old.
  • They rely on poor cellphone photography in bad lighting.
  • They struggle to maintain a consistent social presence.
The Core Problem

As a result, potential customers often have a worse perception of the restaurant online than they do in person. The food is incredible — but the feed makes it look ordinary.

Reframe the problem

New salespeople make the mistake of thinking they're selling "better content." You're not. Understand the real problem:

VisibilityThey aren't being seen
ConsistencyThey can't keep it up
PerceptionThey look smaller than they are

The problem is not content. The problem is visibility. The problem is consistency. The problem is perception. When you frame your pitch around solving those three things, you stop sounding like a videographer and start sounding like a growth partner.

Section 08 · The Conversation

Discovery call framework

The best closers ask the best questions — then listen.

Discovery is not a pitch. It's where you diagnose the problem so your solution feels inevitable. Listen more than you talk — aim for the prospect speaking 70% of the time.

The discovery questions

How are you currently creating content?

Reveals their process, pain, and who's doing the work today.

Who manages your social media?

Owner? A busy manager? A nephew? Tells you how fragile it is.

How often do you post?

Exposes the consistency gap — almost always your wedge.

What marketing is currently working?

Shows what they value and where they already see ROI.

What challenges are you experiencing?

Let them say the problem out loud in their own words.

What goals do you have for the restaurant?

Growth? More covers? A second location? Tie your pitch to it.

What would success look like in six months?

Paints the vision you'll help them reach — and anchors the value.

The Golden Rule

Listen more than you talk. Every problem they name in their own words is a problem you get to solve in your proposal. Take notes and repeat their words back when you present.

Flow of a great discovery call

1

Build rapport (2-3 min)

Warm, human, genuine. Compliment something specific you noticed in your research.

2

Set the frame

"I'd love to learn about your restaurant and your goals — then I'll share if and how we can help."

3

Ask & listen

Work through the discovery questions. Dig deeper on every pain point. Stay curious.

4

Confirm the problem

"So it sounds like the real issue is consistency and how you look online — is that fair?"

5

Bridge to the solution

Only now do you connect Lytmotion to the exact problems they just described.

6

Define next step

Book the follow-up, send the proposal, or — if signals are strong — ask for the business.

Section 11 · The Close

Pricing & packages

Never present price as a cost. Present it as an investment in visibility and growth.

Before you say a number, you must have established value. Pricing only feels expensive in the absence of value. Frame every package around what it produces, not what it contains.

Flagship
Package 1
$3,200 / mo
  • 6–8 Cinematic Reels
  • 10 Professional Photos
  • Monthly strategy & delivery
For brands serious about dominating their market. Enough content to post consistently all month and stay top-of-feed. This is the "we want to grow aggressively" package.
Package 2
$2,000 / mo
  • 4 Cinematic Reels
  • 10 Professional Photos
  • Monthly strategy & delivery
The balanced choice. A steady stream of premium reels and a refreshed photo library every month — consistency without the flagship volume. Most popular starting point.
Package 3
$1,350 / mo
  • 10 Professional Photos
  • Monthly delivery
  • Foundation for the brand
The entry point into a Lytmotion partnership. A monthly injection of premium photography that instantly elevates how the brand looks online — and an easy first "yes" to build on.
Value-Based Framing

Compare the investment to a single thing they already understand. "A handful of extra reservations a month more than covers this — and every piece of content keeps working for you long after we deliver it."

Anchor high. Present Package 1 first. It makes Package 2 feel reasonable and Package 3 feel like a no-brainer entry point.

Language matters. Say "investment," not "cost." Say "package," not "plan." Say "partnership," not "contract." Say "the results this creates," not "what you get."
Section 12 · The Close

Closing techniques

The close isn't a moment — it's the natural result of a great conversation.

If you've diagnosed the problem and built real value, closing is simply helping them make the decision they already want to make.

How to ask for the business

Be direct and calm. Hesitation reads as doubt. Use an assumptive close:

"Based on everything we've talked about, Package 2 is the perfect fit to get you visible and consistent. I have filming availability next week — should we get you on the schedule?"

How to identify buying signals

They ask about logistics

"How does filming day work?" / "When could you start?" — they're picturing it happening.

They ask about specifics

Questions about their dishes, their feed, their schedule mean they're engaged.

They talk in "we" and "us"

"So you'd post these for us?" — they've mentally hired you already.

They go quiet and thoughtful

Silence after value often means they're convincing themselves. Don't fill it.

How to move prospects toward a decision

  • Summarize their own words: "You said you're posting maybe once a week and it's all on your phone — that's exactly what we fix."
  • Offer a clear next step: never end a call without a defined action and date.
  • Reduce the decision: "We can start with Package 3 this month and scale up once you see the response."

How to follow up professionally

  • Follow up within 24 hours of every conversation.
  • Add value each time — a relevant example, a quick idea for their feed.
  • Be persistent without being pushy: 5–7 touch points is normal and professional.
  • Always propose the next step and a specific time.

How to create urgency without pressure

Honest Urgency

Use real reasons: limited filming slots each month, an upcoming busy season worth capturing, the cost of another month of weak content. "Every month without consistent content is a month your competitors are getting discovered instead of you." Never invent fake deadlines — your credibility is worth more than one deal.

Section 14 · Operating

Lytmotion brand standards

You are the brand. Every interaction reflects the quality of our work.

We sell premium, professional presentation. We cannot sell it unless we embody it. Hold yourself to the standard of the content we deliver.

Professional communication

  • Clear, warm, and confident — never pushy or robotic.
  • Free of typos and slang in written outreach. Proofread everything.
  • Lead with value, respect their time, and always be specific.

Response times

  • Reply to prospects and clients within a few hours during business hours.
  • Never leave a warm lead waiting overnight without acknowledgment.
  • Speed signals professionalism and that you value the relationship.

Presentation standards

  • Proposals and decks are clean, branded, and error-free.
  • Show portfolio work that matches the prospect's tier and style.
  • Everything a client sees from you should look as premium as our content.

Dress code when meeting clients

  • Smart, polished, and appropriate to the venue — match the room's level.
  • Fine dining warrants a higher standard than a casual brewery, but always err premium.
  • Well-groomed and put-together. You're the face of a luxury brand.

Representing the brand professionally

Always Remember

You're not a vendor chasing a sale — you're a creative partner helping a business grow. Carry yourself accordingly: confident, generous, and genuinely invested in their success.

Maintaining long-term relationships

  • The sale is the beginning, not the end — clients stay for the relationship.
  • Check in beyond billing. Celebrate their wins and milestones.
  • Happy clients refer other restaurants — your best pipeline is a delighted client.
